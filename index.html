RESEARCH TECHNOLOGIST

Danielle Cuilli (née Schmid) is an ambitious, conscientious researcher with strengths in problem-solving, attention-to-detail, and analytical writing. Her primary professional objective is the identification and clinical application of a data-driven approach to the treatment of genetic, generalized epilepsies. This article will provide a summary of the skills and relevant experience that Danielle has gained in pursuit of this goal.

Introduction

Danielle Cuilli’s interest in epilepsy research began when she was diagnosed with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME) at 14 years-old (1999). At the time of her diagnosis, doctors assured her that her condition could be easily controlled with existing antiseizure medications. However, after several interventions failed to control her symptoms, her epilepsy was eventually labeled as "pharmacoresistant." Although it was no longer expected that her epilepsy would respond to medication, doctors continued to make changes to her treatment in the years that followed.

As a result of her diagnosis, Danielle developed a passionate interest in the physiological manifestations of seizure disorders, and sought to understand both the potential causes of her epilepsy and the pharmacological mechanisms that are traditionally used for its treatment. This interest drove her to pursue a career in neuroscience research, with the goal of identifying cause-based treatments and/or cures for seizure disorders..

In 2019, nearly 20 years after her initial diagnosis, Danielle’s doctors initiated a treatment regimen that resulted in complete seizure control. This development led to a paradigm-shift in Danielle's approach to epilepsy research, which will be further described in the discussion section.

Discussion

Danielle’s interest in personalized medicine is a relatively new component of her long-term goals. Namely, it is the result of the 2019 discovery of a treatment regimen that effectively controlled her seizures.

The drug that put an end to Danielle's seizures was not a novel mechanism of action--in fact, the drug itself had been around for decades before her initial diagnosis. Nonetheless, with over 30 FDA-approved anti-seizure drugs available in the United States, a trial-and-error approach to identifying the best therapy (or combination of drugs) for an individual often consumes a significant portion of a patient's life.

Although physicians are not prescribing these medications "by chance", per se, there is still a great deal of uncertainty surrounding the appropriate course of action. , a vast array of therapeutic options becomes a disadvantage for both the healthcare provider and for the patient.
