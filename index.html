RESEARCH TECHNOLOGIST

Danielle Cuilli (née Schmid) is an ambitious, conscientious researcher with strengths in problem-solving, attention-to-detail, and analytical writing. Her primary professional objective is the identification and clinical application of a data-driven approach to the treatment of genetic, generalized epilepsies. This article will provide a summary of the skills and relevant experience that Danielle has gained in pursuit of this goal.

Introduction

Danielle Cuilli’s interest in epilepsy research began when she was diagnosed with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy at 14 years-old (1999). At the time of her diagnosis, doctors assured her that existing antiseizure medications (ASMs) could readily control her seizures. However, after several pharmacological interventions failed to ameliorate her symptoms, her condition was inevitably described as pharmacoresistant. Although it was no longer expected that her epilepsy would respond to medication, doctors continued to make changes to her treatment in the years that followed.

As a result of her diagnosis, Danielle developed a passionate interest in the physiological manifestations of seizure disorders, and sought to understand both the potential causes of epilepsy and the pharmacological mechanisms that are traditionally used for its treatment. This interest drove her to pursue a career in neuroscience research. She has carefully selected educational opportunities and research positions in effort to enhance her knowledge of the laboratory techniques, experimental designs, and statistical methods that are most relevant to the study of epilepsy.

In 2019, nearly 20 years after her initial diagnosis, Danielle’s doctors initiated a treatment regimen that resulted in complete seizure control. This development led to a paradigm-shift in Danielle's approach to epilepsy research, which will be further reviewed in the discussion section.

Discussion

Danielle’s interest in personalized medicine is a relatively new component of her long-term goals. Namely, it is the result of the 2019 discovery of a treatment regimen that effectively controlled her seizures.

For years, Danielle was convinced that her seizures would only be relieved by a great innovation. What's more: she made it a personal mission to contribute to this discovery; dedicating the better part of her adult life establishing a career in this field. As it turns out, however, the drug that put an end to Danielle's seizures was not a novel mechanism of action. In fact, the drug itself had been available for decades even before her initial diagnosis.

There are over 30 FDA-approved anti-seizure drugs available in the United States. This vast array of of therapeutic options has essentially become a disadvantage, as the process of identifying the best treatment for an individual patient can often consume a significant portion of a patient's life. Although physicians are not prescribing these medications "by chance", per se, the grim outlook for patients with pharmacoresistant epilepsy suggests that the standard of care needs serious review. As a result of this Discovery has little value if it cannot be harnessed at the appropriate time. It is time for medicine to abandon guesswork, and instead search for patterns in the data that already exists. These data can be used

- calculate and discuss the probability of choosing “the one working drug” by chance>, - With over 30 FDA-approved drugs, …these will obviously be very small p values, ergo, we need to NOT do this by chance. We need to identify patterns—i.e., Danielle wants to get into data science.
